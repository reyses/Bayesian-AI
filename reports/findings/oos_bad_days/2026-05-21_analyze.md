# OOS Bad-Day Lift -- Intraday Session-Stop (ANALYZE)

DMAIC ANALYZE phase. Project goal: lift the OOS bad days. This document tests one rule family -- an intraday session-stop -- and reports whether it behaves differently from the previously-rejected per-trade drawdown stop.

- **Date:** 2026-05-21
- **IS:** 275 days, per-leg FLAT P&L from `is_hardened_legs.csv`
- **OOS:** 51 sealed days, from `oos_hardened_legs_full.csv`
- **Strategy:** zigzag FLAT baseline, 1-contract, no trade management (honest per-leg labels)
- **Leg-to-hour attribution:** each leg assigned to the ET (America/New_York) hour-of-day of its `entry_ts`.
- **Rule:** if cumulative session P&L through ET hour H is <= T, drop all legs entered after hour H on that day.

## 0. Rule mechanics

For each day: `early = sum(pnl of legs with entry_hour <= H)`, `late = sum(pnl of legs with entry_hour > H)`. The day **triggers** when `early <= T`. On a triggered day the rule keeps `early` and drops `late`; on a non-triggered day the rule P&L equals FLAT (`early + late`). So the rule's per-day delta vs FLAT is exactly `-late` on triggered days and `0` otherwise. **The rule helps only when `late < 0` on triggered days** -- i.e. when a bad morning genuinely predicts a bad afternoon.

### 2a. Is the neg-day count honest? (the illusion check)

The IS selection criterion was "reduce negative-day count". But with T = $0 a day stops being "negative" the instant the rule halts it at a small loss -- the day is flattened to a P&L in `[T, 0]`, i.e. it lands at break-even-or-tiny-loss and drops out of the `< 0` bucket *without actually being a winning day*. Decompose what the rule did to OOS days:

- FLAT-negative days that the rule made non-negative: **0**.
  - of those, **0** turned genuinely PROFITABLE (rule P&L > $0).
  - and **0** are COSMETIC -- the day still lost or broke even, it just no longer counts as `< 0` because the rule halted it at the threshold.
- FLAT-positive days the rule turned NEGATIVE (created losers by cutting a profitable afternoon): **6**.

**Read: the rule rescued ZERO bad days and CREATED 6 new ones.** The IS criterion picked T = $0 because in-sample it shaved the count by reclassifying small losers as break-even -- but on the sealed OOS that reclassification effect vanished and only the damage remained: the rule turned 6 profitable afternoons into losses, so OOS negative days went 14 -> 20 (the WRONG direction). The 'neg-day reduction' the rule was selected for does not exist out of sample.

## 4. Honest framing -- session stop vs per-trade drawdown stop

The per-TRADE drawdown stop ("cut a leg at -$100 open P&L") was REJECTED: 76% of -$100-drawdown legs recovered, and a predictive model had AUC 0.465 there -- worse than a coin. The failure mode: the cut is taken precisely on the population that mostly recovers.

The session-level analog asks the same question one level up: when a day's morning (through hour H) is at/below T, does the **rest of the day** recover (close hours > H positive)? Two recovery numbers matter:

- **IS, the chosen cell (H=13, T=$+0.00): 39%** of triggered days recovered in the afternoon. (Grid-wide the recovery rate spans 33-52%, mean 42%.)
- **OOS, the same cell: 81%** of the 16 triggered days recovered (mean rest-of-day $+252).

Two things to be honest about:

1. **Even the IS recovery rate (39%) does not make the stop pay.** A sub-50% rate means the afternoon is negative more often than not -- yet the net IS $/day delta is still $-3. The reason: recovery rate is a *count*; the dollars are asymmetric. The afternoons that recover are larger than the afternoons that lose, so cutting on a coin-ish trigger throws away positive expected value even when more than half the afternoons are red. Same dollar-asymmetry trap as the per-trade stop.
2. **OOS makes it worse, not better:** on the sealed sample the recovery rate jumped to 81% -- the bad mornings were followed by *good* afternoons even more often than IS. The trigger has no real predictive content; it just halts the day at a point that was, in the OOS window, usually followed by a profitable recovery.

**Verdict is written in section 5.**

## 5. Verdict

- **The intraday session-stop FAILS the same way the per-trade drawdown stop failed.** The trigger fires on a population that, dollar-weighted, recovers: the chosen cell's IS recovery rate is 39% and its net IS $/day delta is only $-3 -- it never paid even in-sample.
- **The IS "negative-day reduction" does not survive OOS.** On the sealed sample the rule rescued 0 FLAT-negative days into profit, reclassified 0 as merely break-even, and CREATED 6 brand-new losing days by cutting profitable afternoons -- net OOS negative days 14 -> 20, the wrong direction. The IS count drop (80 -> 60) was T = $0 flattening small losers into the `[T, 0]` band so they exit the `< 0` bucket without becoming wins; that cosmetic effect did not generalize and the OOS rule simply adds losers.
- **On sealed OOS the chosen rule (H=13, T=$+0.00) is a significant LOSS:** mean $/day $+454 -> $+375, delta $-79 (95% CI [$-154, $-22], SIGNIFICANT (CI strictly < 0)). The worst single day got WORSE ($-571 -> $-844) and daily P&L std went UP ($+742 -> $+787). The rule de-risks nothing -- it adds risk while removing money.
- **The fixed RTH-only windows do not lift the OOS bad days either.** Both 09-15 and 08-16 trim 3 OOS negative days, but every OOS $/day delta CI includes zero (not significant) and the point estimate is negative. RTH-only does cut daily P&L std meaningfully -- it is a defensible variance-reduction trade -- but it is not a bad-day fix and it is not free.

### Bottom line

**An intraday session-stop does NOT lift the OOS bad days -- it fails exactly like the rejected per-trade drawdown stop.** Bad mornings do not predict bad afternoons in dollar terms; the trigger cuts a recovering population. The only honest lever in this family is unconditional RTH-only trading as a *variance* reduction (lower std, ~3 fewer negative days), and even that costs a non-significant ~$27-45/day of mean P&L. The bad-day problem is NOT solvable with a same-day cumulative-P&L stop; a genuine ex-ante signal (pre-open / cross-day) is required.
